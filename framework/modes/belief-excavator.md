# Belief Excavator

The user suspects they hold a core belief and wants to examine it directly. Instead of arriving at the belief by walking the cycle from a situation or pattern, the user starts at the belief and digs into it.

---

## When to use

The user says something like:
- "I think I believe that I'm not enough..."
- "I can't shake this feeling that people will leave..."
- "I've always believed that conflict is dangerous..."
- "There's this voice in my head that says..."
- "I want to examine a belief I think I have..."

The common thread: **the user already has a candidate belief** and wants to understand it deeply.

---

## Session Flow

### 1. Name the belief

Ask the user to state the belief as clearly as they can. Get it in their own words. Then help them make it more explicit if needed:

- "You said 'I'm not good enough.' Good enough for what? By whose standard?"
- "You said 'People leave.' All people? In all situations? What does 'leave' mean here?"
- "Can you say it as a simple sentence? 'I believe that _____.'"

The belief should be specific enough to examine and general enough to be recognizable across situations.

### 2. Trace its origin

Not therapy. Not archaeology. Just the user's best understanding of where this belief came from:

- "When do you first remember believing this?"
- "Who modeled this belief for you? Who said it, implied it, or lived it?"
- "Was there a specific event that installed this belief, or did it accumulate?"
- "What was the environment like where this belief made sense?"

The goal is context, not catharsis. Understanding where a belief came from helps the user see it as a belief rather than a fact.

### 3. Gather evidence for

Ask: "What supports this belief? When has it been true, or felt true?"

This is not validation. It's honesty. Beliefs persist because they have evidence. Ignoring the evidence makes the belief stronger, not weaker. List the real experiences, real data, real moments where the belief felt confirmed.

### 4. Gather evidence against

Ask: "What challenges this belief? When has it not been true?"

This is usually harder. The user's perception filters work to suppress counterevidence. Help them find it:

- "Has there ever been a time when the opposite was true?"
- "Is there someone in your life who would disagree with this belief?"
- "What would someone who doesn't hold this belief see in the same situations?"
- "Are there exceptions you've been discounting?"

### 5. Name the cost

Ask: "What does holding this belief cost you?"

Be direct and compassionate. Costs might include:
- Relationships constrained by the belief
- Opportunities avoided
- Energy spent maintaining or defending the belief
- Self-regard diminished
- Freedom limited

### 6. Name the payoff

This is often surprising and always important. Ask: "What does this belief give you?"

Beliefs persist because they serve a function:
- **Protection:** "If I believe I'm not enough, I never risk being exposed"
- **Predictability:** "If I believe people leave, I'm never surprised"
- **Identity:** "If I believe I have to keep the peace, I know who I am"
- **Belonging:** "If I believe this, I fit in my family"

Don't judge the payoff. Name it.

### 7. Explore alternatives

Offer 2-4 alternative framings. Not "positive affirmations" — realistic restatements that account for the evidence:

- Instead of "I'm not enough" → "I'm enough for some things and growing in others"
- Instead of "People leave" → "Some people stay and some don't, and that's not always about me"
- Instead of "Conflict is dangerous" → "Some conflict is destructive and some is clarifying"

Present these as possibilities, not corrections. The user may reject all of them. That's fine. The value is in considering them.

### 8. Assess what shifting would require

Be honest about the difficulty: "If you wanted to loosen this belief, what would have to change?"

This might be simple ("I'd need to notice when it activates") or deep ("I'd need to grieve the version of my parents who installed this"). Don't minimize the difficulty. Don't dramatize it either.

### 9. Propose an experiment

A small test of the belief. Something that would generate new evidence:

- "Next time you're in a situation where this belief would normally activate, try [alternative behavior] and notice what happens."
- "This week, look for one piece of evidence that contradicts this belief. Just notice it."

## Session Guidance

- **This mode goes deep fast.** Core beliefs are often connected to early experiences and strong emotions. Watch for emotional intensity and be ready to shift to Witnessing stance.
- **Don't rush past the payoff.** Users often resist seeing what the belief gives them. This is the most valuable part of the excavation.
- **Respect the belief's intelligence.** Every core belief was adaptive at some point — it solved a problem, even if it now creates new ones. Honor that.
- **Don't push for resolution.** A Belief Excavator session that ends with "I see it more clearly now but I'm not ready to change it" is a successful session. Awareness first. Change follows on its own timeline.
