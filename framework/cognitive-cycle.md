# The Cognitive Cycle

The cognitive cycle is the engine of this framework. It describes the causal loop through which a person's inner life produces their outer life and feeds back into itself. Every mode, every question, every output schema is in service of helping someone see where they are on this loop and how they got there.

The cycle is not a theory to be debated. It is a map to be used. Like any map, it simplifies. The territory is messier. But the map is useful precisely because it gives you a way to point at something and say: *that — that is what is happening right now.*

## The Loop

```
Beliefs → Perceptions → Thoughts → Emotions → Feelings → Decisions → Actions → Behaviors → Consequences → Experiences → (back to Beliefs)
```

The cycle is continuous. There is no true starting point. A person can enter awareness at any node. The tool's job is to help them see the full loop from wherever they enter.

---

## Nodes

### Beliefs

**Definition:** The deep assumptions a person holds about themselves, others, and the world. Often unexamined. Often inherited. Often operating below the threshold of awareness. Beliefs are not opinions — they are the ground the opinions stand on.

**Characteristic markers:**
- "I always..." / "I never..."
- "People are..." / "The world is..."
- "That's just how I am"
- "I have to..." / "I can't..."
- "I don't deserve..." / "I'm not the kind of person who..."
- Statements that feel like facts rather than interpretations

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What assumption would have to be true for you to react the way you did?
- If you traced this reaction all the way back, what would you find at the root?
- What do you believe about yourself in situations like this?
- What would someone who knew you well say you believe about [this topic]?
- When did you first start believing this?

**Feeds:** Perceptions (beliefs act as filters on what we notice and how we interpret it)
**Fed by:** Experiences (what we live through reinforces or occasionally challenges our beliefs)

---

### Perceptions

**Definition:** The filtered version of reality that beliefs produce. Not what happened, but what you noticed and how you interpreted it. Two people in the same room perceive different rooms because their belief filters are different.

**Characteristic markers:**
- Selective attention: noticing what confirms existing beliefs
- Interpretation bias: reading neutral events as confirming fears or assumptions
- "I could tell that..." / "It was obvious that..."
- "They were clearly..." (confident attribution of others' inner states)
- Ignoring or discounting evidence that contradicts the belief

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What did you notice in that situation? What might you have missed?
- If someone with the opposite assumption were in that room, what would they have noticed?
- What are you interpreting vs. what did you actually observe?
- Is there a version of this that's neutral rather than personal?
- What evidence did you discount?

**Feeds:** Thoughts (perceptions become the raw material for the stories we tell ourselves)
**Fed by:** Beliefs (what we believe determines what we perceive)

---

### Thoughts

**Definition:** The narrative layer. The stories, explanations, justifications, and predictions the mind generates in response to what it perceives. Thoughts feel like thinking, but they are often more like narration — the mind telling you a story about what's happening.

**Characteristic markers:**
- Inner monologue or mental chatter
- Storytelling: "What happened was..." / "The reason is..."
- Rumination: replaying events with commentary
- Future-casting: "This means that..." / "Next time they'll..."
- Justification: "I had no choice because..."
- Catastrophizing or minimizing

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What story are you telling yourself about this?
- If you wrote down the thought as a sentence, what would it say?
- Is this thought a fact, an interpretation, or a prediction?
- Whose voice does this thought sound like?
- What would you think about this situation if you had no history with it?

**Feeds:** Emotions (the story we tell ourselves generates the emotional response)
**Fed by:** Perceptions (what we noticed becomes the material for the narrative)

---

### Emotions

**Definition:** The reactive response to the story. Emotions are fast, categorical, and often feel like they *are* the situation rather than a response to it. The main ones relevant to self-inquiry: anger, fear, shame, sadness, joy, disgust, surprise. But also subtler ones: resentment, jealousy, guilt, relief, contempt, tenderness.

**Characteristic markers:**
- "I felt angry/hurt/scared/ashamed"
- Emotional flooding: the feeling is so strong it becomes the whole experience
- Emotional mislabeling: calling anger "frustration" or fear "concern" to soften it
- "I don't know what I'm feeling" (often the most important thing to explore)
- Emotional absence: "I didn't feel anything" (often dissociation or suppression)

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What emotion showed up? Can you name it precisely?
- Where does that emotion sit on a scale from mild to overwhelming?
- Is this the surface emotion or is there something underneath it?
- Have you felt this exact emotion in other, seemingly unrelated situations?
- What would it mean about you if you let yourself fully feel this?

**Feeds:** Feelings (emotions produce bodily sensations and somatic responses)
**Fed by:** Thoughts (the narrative triggers the emotional reaction)

---

### Feelings

**Definition:** The bodily, somatic dimension. Where emotion lives in the body. Feelings are the physical experience of the emotional response: the tight chest, the clenched jaw, the knot in the stomach, the heat in the face. This node is often the most neglected because our culture privileges thinking over sensing.

**Characteristic markers:**
- Tension, constriction, heaviness, heat, cold, numbness
- "My stomach dropped" / "My chest got tight" / "I felt sick"
- Restlessness, inability to sit still
- Fatigue or sudden exhaustion
- The body "bracing" for something
- Physical shutdown: going numb, feeling nothing

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Where in your body do you feel this?
- If the sensation had a shape, color, or temperature, what would it be?
- Is the sensation moving or static?
- What does your body seem to want to do right now? (run, fight, freeze, collapse)
- When you've felt this physical sensation before, what was happening?

**Feeds:** Decisions (the body's signals often drive what we decide to do, consciously or not)
**Fed by:** Emotions (emotional responses produce bodily sensations)

---

### Decisions

**Definition:** The choice point. Often experienced as conscious deliberation, but frequently automatic — the decision has already been made by the time you notice it. Decisions are where the inner cycle begins to become the outer cycle: the bridge between what you feel and what you do.

**Characteristic markers:**
- "I decided to..." (conscious)
- "I just found myself..." (automatic)
- "I had no choice" (a decision disguised as a fact)
- Avoidance as a decision: choosing not to act is still a choice
- "Someone had to..." / "I couldn't just..."
- The moment right before the action, often invisible in retrospect

**Diagnostic questions:**
- At what point did you decide what to do? Or did it feel automatic?
- What options did you consider? What options didn't you consider?
- What were you trying to protect, avoid, or achieve with this choice?
- If you could go back to that decision point, what else could you have done?
- What would you have decided if you weren't afraid?

**Feeds:** Actions (decisions produce specific behaviors)
**Fed by:** Feelings (somatic signals drive choice, often below awareness)

---

### Actions

**Definition:** What you actually did. Not what you meant to do, not what you planned to do, not what you wish you'd done. The observable behavior. Actions are where the internal cycle becomes external and visible to others.

**Characteristic markers:**
- The specific thing said, done, or not done
- Often different from the intended action
- "I snapped at them" / "I went quiet" / "I left the room"
- "I said I was fine" (action that contradicts the inner state)
- Inaction as action: what you didn't do that you could have

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What did you actually do? (Not what you meant to — what you did.)
- How would a camera in the room have recorded your behavior?
- Was there a gap between what you intended and what you did?
- What did you not do that was also an option?
- If someone you respect watched you in that moment, what would they have seen?

**Feeds:** Behaviors (repeated actions become patterns)
**Fed by:** Decisions (choices produce specific actions)

---

### Behaviors

**Definition:** Repeated action patterns. When an action happens once, it's an action. When it happens consistently across situations, it's a behavior. Behaviors are where individual choices calcify into habits, tendencies, and character.

**Characteristic markers:**
- "I always do this" / "This is what I do"
- Others can predict the behavior: "I knew you'd say that"
- The behavior feels automatic, effortless, inevitable
- Resistance to changing the behavior despite wanting to
- Rationalizing the behavior as personality: "I'm just a [anxious/avoidant/controlling] person"

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Is this something you do once, or is it a pattern?
- In what other situations does this same behavior show up?
- Who first modeled this behavior for you?
- What would change in your life if this behavior stopped?
- What does this behavior protect you from?

**Feeds:** Consequences (behaviors produce predictable outcomes over time)
**Fed by:** Actions (repeated actions become behavioral patterns)

---

### Consequences

**Definition:** What results from the behavior. Not punishment or reward — just what happens. Consequences can be external (how others respond, what opportunities open or close) or internal (how the behavior affects your own sense of self, your energy, your relationships over time).

**Characteristic markers:**
- The outcome that keeps happening: "Every time I do this, they..."
- Relationship patterns: "People always end up..."
- Self-fulfilling prophecies: the consequence confirms the original belief
- "I don't understand why this keeps happening to me"
- The gap between intended and actual outcomes

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What happened as a result of what you did?
- Is this the outcome you wanted? If not, what were you actually hoping for?
- What is the pattern of consequences in situations like this?
- How do others typically respond when you behave this way?
- If you kept doing this for another five years, what would your life look like?

**Feeds:** Experiences (consequences become the raw material for how we interpret what happened)
**Fed by:** Behaviors (patterns of action produce patterns of outcome)

---

### Experiences

**Definition:** How you interpret and internalize what happened. Not the event itself, but the meaning you make of it. Experiences are where consequences become stories, and stories become the soil that beliefs grow in. This is the node where the cycle completes: the experience feeds back into beliefs, either reinforcing them or — rarely, with effort — updating them.

**Characteristic markers:**
- "What I learned from that is..."
- "That just proves that..."
- "See? I was right about..."
- "I'll never do that again" (experience interpreted as a fixed lesson)
- "That was just a fluke" (dismissing disconfirming experience)

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What did you take away from this experience?
- What belief did this experience reinforce? Did it challenge any?
- If someone else had the exact same experience, might they interpret it differently?
- Is the lesson you're drawing the only possible lesson?
- What would it mean if this experience were neutral — neither proof nor disproof of anything?

**Feeds:** Beliefs (experiences reinforce or occasionally update the beliefs that started the cycle)
**Fed by:** Consequences (what happened becomes what it meant)

---

## How the Tool Uses This

1. **Entry point detection.** The user enters the conversation somewhere on the cycle. The tool's first job is to recognize where. Someone saying "I keep avoiding conflict" enters at Behaviors. Someone saying "I felt this rage" enters at Emotions. Someone saying "I believe I'm not enough" enters at Beliefs.

2. **Walking backwards.** From the entry point, walk backwards around the cycle to surface what fed the current node. If entry is at Actions, walk back through Decisions, Feelings, Emotions, Thoughts, Perceptions, to Beliefs. The backwards walk answers: *what is driving this?*

3. **Walking forwards.** From the entry point, walk forwards to surface what the current node produces. If entry is at Beliefs, walk forward through Perceptions, Thoughts, Emotions, all the way to Experiences feeding back to Beliefs. The forwards walk answers: *what does this lead to?*

4. **Naming the loop.** Once the full cycle is mapped for a specific situation or pattern, name it. A loop is a complete circuit: a belief that produces perceptions that produce thoughts that produce emotions that produce feelings that drive decisions that produce actions that become behaviors that create consequences that become experiences that reinforce the belief. The loop is the unit of insight.

5. **Proposing an experiment.** A small, concrete, doable thing the user can try to interrupt the loop at one node. Not a life change. A single experiment. "Next time you notice [the thought], try [the interruption] and notice what happens."
