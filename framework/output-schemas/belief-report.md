# Belief Report — Output Schema

The output of Belief Excavator mode. A deep examination of a single core belief: what supports it, what challenges it, what it costs, and what alternatives exist.

# Belief Report: "{{The belief, in the user's words}}"

## The Belief

{{The belief stated clearly, in the user's own language. Then a restatement in more explicit terms if the user's version was euphemistic or indirect. "You said '...'. A more direct way to say it might be: '...'"}}

## Where It Comes From

{{What the user knows or suspects about the origin. Family messages, early experiences, cultural context, formative events. This is not archaeological certainty — it's the user's best understanding. "You trace this to..." or "This seems connected to..."}}

## Evidence For

{{What supports this belief being true? Real experiences, real data, real patterns that make this belief feel justified. The point is not to validate the belief but to honor the fact that beliefs persist because they have evidence. List as bullet points.}}

## Evidence Against

{{What challenges this belief? Experiences, people, or situations where the belief didn't hold. Exceptions, contradictions, counterexamples. Often harder for the user to generate — the tool may need to help surface these. List as bullet points.}}

## The Cost

{{What does holding this belief cost the user? In relationships, opportunities, energy, self-regard, freedom. "As long as you hold this belief, you..." This section should be honest but compassionate. The cost is the reason to examine the belief — not to shame the user for holding it.}}

## The Payoff

{{What does the belief give the user? Beliefs persist because they serve a function — protection, predictability, identity, belonging. "This belief might be giving you..." This is often the most surprising section for the user. Name the payoff without judgment.}}

## Alternative Framings

{{2-4 alternative beliefs that could replace or soften this one. Not "positive affirmations" — realistic alternatives that account for the evidence. Each framing should feel honest, not aspirational.}}

- "{{Alternative 1}}" — {{Why this might be more accurate or useful}}
- "{{Alternative 2}}" — {{Why this might be more accurate or useful}}

## What Shifting Would Require

{{What would have to change — internally or externally — for this belief to loosen its grip? Not a prescription. An honest assessment of the difficulty. "Shifting this belief would probably require..." or "This belief is unlikely to change unless..."}}

## Experiment

**Try this:** {{A small experiment to test the belief.}}

**Notice:** {{What to pay attention to.}}

**When:** {{Timeframe.}}

---

## Guidelines

- The belief should be stated in the user's own words first. Always.
- Evidence For is not agreement — it's honesty. Beliefs that lack real evidence are weaker and easier to examine. Beliefs with real evidence are stronger and harder to shift. Both are worth seeing clearly.
- Evidence Against requires the most work from the tool. Users often can't see counterevidence on their own. This is where Socratic questioning earns its keep.
- The Cost and Payoff sections together explain why the belief persists. Neither alone is sufficient.
- Alternative Framings are not replacements. They are possibilities. The user may reject all of them. That's fine.
- Never tell the user which alternative to adopt. Present options. Let them choose.
