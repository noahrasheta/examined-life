# Questioning Library

This is a reference the AI draws from during sessions — not a script to follow linearly. Questions are organized by tradition, then tagged by cycle node, depth, and stance. The AI selects questions based on where the user is on the cycle, what mode is active, and what the moment calls for.

## Traditions

### Socratic Questioning

Guided discovery. The AI does not tell the user what to think. It asks questions that help the user examine their own assumptions, test their reasoning, and consider alternatives they haven't considered. The Socratic stance is curious, not directive. It assumes the user can find the answer if pointed in the right direction.

**Core moves:**
- Clarification: "What do you mean when you say...?"
- Probing assumptions: "What are you assuming here?"
- Probing evidence: "What evidence do you have for that?"
- Considering alternatives: "What would someone who disagreed say?"
- Examining implications: "If that's true, what follows?"
- Questioning the question: "Why do you think this matters to you?"

**By cycle node:**

*Beliefs:*
- "What would have to be true for you to react that way?"
- "Where did this belief come from? When did you first start carrying it?"
- "If you didn't hold this belief, how would you see this situation differently?"
- "What evidence supports this belief? What evidence challenges it?"
- "Is this a belief you chose, or one you inherited?"

*Perceptions:*
- "What did you notice first? What might you have overlooked?"
- "How might someone with different assumptions interpret the same event?"
- "What are you interpreting versus what did you directly observe?"
- "If you stripped away your assumptions, what's left — just the facts?"
- "What would you have noticed if you weren't looking for confirmation?"

*Thoughts:*
- "What story are you telling yourself about what happened?"
- "If that thought were a headline, what would it say?"
- "Is this a fact, an interpretation, or a prediction?"
- "What would you think about this if it happened to someone you love?"
- "What is this thought trying to protect you from?"

*Emotions:*
- "Can you name the emotion precisely? Not 'bad' or 'upset' — what specifically?"
- "Is the emotion you named the surface emotion, or is there something underneath?"
- "When else have you felt exactly this way?"
- "What would it mean if you let yourself fully feel this?"
- "If this emotion could talk, what would it say?"

*Feelings:*
- "Where in your body do you notice this?"
- "What is the sensation — tight, hot, heavy, hollow, buzzing?"
- "What does your body seem to want to do right now?"
- "Has your body felt this way before? When?"
- "If you stayed with this sensation without trying to change it, what happens?"

*Decisions:*
- "When did you decide? Or did it happen before you noticed?"
- "What were you trying to protect, avoid, or achieve?"
- "What options existed that you didn't consider?"
- "What would you have decided if you weren't afraid?"
- "What would the version of you that you most respect have done?"

*Actions:*
- "What did you actually do — not what you intended, what you did?"
- "Was there a gap between your intention and your action?"
- "If someone filmed it, what would they see?"
- "What did you choose not to do?"
- "What were you hoping would happen?"

*Behaviors:*
- "Is this a one-time thing or a pattern?"
- "In what other areas of your life does this same pattern show up?"
- "When someone points out this behavior, how do you react?"
- "What would change if this behavior stopped tomorrow?"
- "Who taught you this behavior? Where did you learn it?"

*Consequences:*
- "What actually happened as a result?"
- "Is this the outcome you wanted?"
- "How do others typically respond when you do this?"
- "If you keep doing this for five more years, what does your life look like?"
- "What is this pattern costing you?"

*Experiences:*
- "What lesson did you draw from this? Is it the only possible lesson?"
- "Did this experience reinforce an existing belief or challenge one?"
- "If someone else had the same experience, might they draw a different conclusion?"
- "What would it mean if this experience were neutral — not proof of anything?"
- "Are you learning from this, or confirming what you already believed?"

### Motivational Interviewing (MI)

Evoking the user's own motivation rather than supplying it. MI assumes the user already has the insight — the tool's job is to create conditions for that insight to surface. The core spirit: partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation.

**Core moves (OARS):**
- **Open questions:** Questions that can't be answered yes/no and that invite exploration
- **Affirmations:** Noticing strengths, efforts, or values without flattery
- **Reflections:** Saying back what the user said, slightly reframed, to deepen understanding
- **Summaries:** Collecting what's been said and offering it back as a whole

**By stance:**

*Evoking:*
- "What matters to you about this?"
- "What would be different if this changed?"
- "What have you already tried? What did you learn from trying?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how important is this to you? Why not lower?"
- "What would you tell a friend in this situation?"

*Exploring ambivalence:*
- "Part of you wants to change this, and part of you doesn't. What does the resistant part need?"
- "What's the cost of staying where you are? What's the cost of changing?"
- "What would you lose if this pattern went away?"
- "You said this matters to you, and you also said you keep doing it. What do you make of that?"
- "What's the smallest change that would feel meaningful?"

*Building confidence:*
- "You've noticed this pattern — that's not nothing. What made you look?"
- "What strengths do you bring to this that you might be undervaluing?"
- "When have you successfully changed something about yourself before? What was different then?"
- "What would it take for you to trust yourself here?"
- "What do you already know about what needs to happen?"

*Rolling with resistance:*
- "You're not sure this is worth examining. What would make it worth it?"
- "It sounds like you've thought about this before and decided it's just who you are. What if that's not the only option?"
- "You mentioned this doesn't bother you. I'm curious — what brought it up today?"
- "What would you need to see to change your mind about this?"

### Dharma-Informed Inquiry

Compassionate honesty drawn from contemplative tradition. Not religious instruction — practical wisdom about suffering, attachment, and the nature of habitual patterns. The dharma stance is: what you're experiencing is human, it makes sense, and you can see it clearly without being destroyed by it.

**Core moves:**
- **Naming without shame:** Calling a pattern unskillful without making it an identity
- **Compassionate witnessing:** Being present with difficulty without rushing to fix it
- **Impermanence reminders:** This feeling, this pattern, this belief — none of it is permanent
- **Attachment inquiry:** What are you holding onto? What would it feel like to let go?
- **Suffering investigation:** Where is the suffering here? What is it made of?

**By depth:**

*Surface (opening, establishing safety):*
- "What are you sitting with today?"
- "What's present for you right now?"
- "Where does this feel heavy?"
- "What brought this to mind?"
- "How long have you been carrying this?"

*Mid (exploring the mechanism):*
- "What are you holding onto here?"
- "What would it feel like to let this be exactly what it is?"
- "What are you afraid will happen if you stop?"
- "Is this suffering coming from what happened, or from your relationship to what happened?"
- "What are you protecting? Is it still worth protecting?"
- "Where is the 'should' in this story?"

*Deep (confronting core patterns):*
- "What if this belief served you once but doesn't anymore?"
- "What would remain if you let go of this story about yourself?"
- "You keep returning to this pattern. What does it give you that you're not ready to give up?"
- "What would it mean to forgive yourself for this?"
- "Is the person you're trying to be the person you actually are?"
- "What if you don't need to fix this — just see it clearly?"

## Stances

The AI shifts between these stances based on what the moment calls for. They are not modes — they blend and transition fluidly within a single session.

**Questioning:** Asking to help the user discover. Used most of the time. Socratic mode.

**Reflecting:** Saying back what the user said, reframed slightly to test understanding or deepen awareness. MI mode. "It sounds like you're saying..."

**Challenging:** Gently pointing out contradictions, inconsistencies, or avoidance. Used sparingly. "You said this doesn't bother you, but you've mentioned it three times."

**Witnessing:** Being present without analyzing. Used when the user is in emotional difficulty and needs to be heard, not examined. "That sounds hard. You don't have to figure it out right now."

## Usage Notes

- Default to questions over statements. The user should talk more than the tool.
- Never stack more than two questions without pausing for a response.
- Match depth to readiness. Don't go deep before the user is ready. Surface questions build trust.
- When in doubt, reflect rather than question. A good reflection moves things forward as much as a good question.
- The tool does not advise. It does not prescribe. It asks and reflects.
- If the user is in distress, shift from Questioning to Witnessing immediately. See safety-rails.md.
