## Recurring Patterns

### Conflict avoidance loop

- **Description:** Anticipating negative reaction -> suppressing needs -> accumulating resentment -> distance -> confirming belief that relationships are fragile
- **Appears in:** [[2026-04-07-pattern-finder-conflict-avoidance]], [[2026-04-25-situation-examiner-friend-cancellation]], [[2026-05-02-communication-mirror-email-to-team]]
- **Typical cycle path:** Belief ("I'm a burden") -> Perception (scanning for rejection) -> Thought (predicting catastrophe) -> Fear -> Bracing -> Silence -> Distance -> "See, they're pulling away"
- **Variations:** With partner: silence and "I'm fine." With friends: not reaching out. At work: over-qualifying every request in emails.

### Productivity-shame spiral

- **Description:** Setting impossibly high standards -> failing to meet them -> shame -> avoidance -> more shame -> confirming "I'm not enough"
- **Appears in:** [[2026-04-10-situation-examiner-meeting-feedback]], [[2026-05-10-pattern-finder-procrastination-loop]]
- **Typical cycle path:** Belief ("my value = my output") -> Perception (noticing only what's incomplete) -> Thought ("I should be further along") -> Shame -> Heaviness/fatigue -> Avoidance -> Nothing produced -> "See, I can't do this"
- **Variations:** At work: procrastination. In creative projects: never finishing. In learning: feeling behind.

## Themes

- **Self-worth as conditional.** Most sessions orbit around the question of whether the user's value is inherent or earned. The "burden" belief and the "production" belief are two faces of the same coin: "I am only acceptable when I am useful and invisible."
- **Predicted catastrophe vs. actual experience.** In every session where the user predicted a negative reaction from others, the predicted catastrophe either didn't happen or was much milder than expected. The prediction engine is overactive.
- **The body knows first.** The user consistently reports physical sensations (chest tightness, stomach clenching, fatigue) before they can name the emotion. The somatic entry point might be the most reliable self-awareness channel.

## Shifts and Growth

- The "burden" belief is now being named in real-time. In early sessions, it took extensive backward walking to surface. By late April, the user was catching it themselves: "There it is again — the burden thing." Recognition speed is a growth marker.
- The experiment from the April 7 session (saying what's actually bothering them instead of "I'm fine") was attempted twice. First attempt: partner responded with curiosity, not defensiveness. Second attempt: brief tension followed by a closer conversation. Neither matched the catastrophic prediction. The user noted: "The worst case in reality is better than the best case in my head."
- The May 2 Communication Mirror session produced a visible shift in email tone — the user reported rewriting an email to be more direct after seeing how many qualifiers they were using.

## Blind Spots

- We haven't explored anger. The user references frustration and resentment but never names anger directly. When resentment "builds up and I explode," what emotion is driving the explosion? This might be worth a session.
- The origin of the "burden" belief hasn't been fully excavated. The user mentioned family valuing being "low-maintenance" but hasn't gone deeper. A Belief Excavator session focused on this belief's origin might be valuable when the user is ready.
- Joy and satisfaction are absent from the sessions so far. Every session has been about friction or difficulty. What does the user enjoy? When do they feel most themselves? A Life Review might surface this.

## Suggested Directions

1. **A Belief Excavator session on "My value comes from what I produce."** This belief is newer to the surface and hasn't been examined as deeply as the burden belief. It's connected to work, creativity, and self-worth in ways that seem worth mapping.
2. **A Life Review.** Seven sessions in, there's enough data for a wider-angle view. The user might benefit from examining roles, values alignment, and what's working — not just what's stuck.
3. **Exploring anger.** Not as a problem to solve, but as an emotion to get acquainted with. What is the user's relationship with anger? Where does it go when it's suppressed?
