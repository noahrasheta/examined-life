# Cognitive Loop Map: Conflict Avoidance in Close Relationships

## What brought this up

"I keep avoiding conflict with my partner. Every time something bothers me, I just swallow it. Then it builds up and I either explode or shut down completely. I know I do it but I can't seem to stop."

## The Loop

### Entry: Behaviors

The user entered at Behaviors — describing a repeated pattern of conflict avoidance that they recognize but can't seem to interrupt.

### Walking Back

#### Actions

When something bothers the user, they consistently choose silence. They report saying "I'm fine" or changing the subject. In the most recent instance, their partner asked if something was wrong after dinner, and the user said "No, just tired" despite feeling hurt about a comment made earlier.

#### Decisions

The decision to stay quiet appears to happen before conscious awareness. "By the time I notice, I've already said I'm fine." When asked what they're trying to protect, the user said: "The relationship. If I say what's bothering me, it'll turn into a fight, and then things will be bad between us."

#### Feelings

Tightness in the chest and a "clenching" sensation in the stomach when the moment of potential conflict arrives. The user described it as "bracing."

#### Emotions

Fear — specifically, fear of the other person's reaction. Underneath the fear: shame. "If I need things to be different, something's wrong with me for needing that."

#### Thoughts

"If I bring this up, they'll get defensive. Then it'll escalate. Then they'll think I'm too much. Then they'll start pulling away."

The user noted this is a prediction, not a memory of what actually happened. When asked how many times the predicted catastrophe has actually occurred, they said: "Honestly? Almost never. But it feels like it will every time."

#### Perceptions

Hypervigilance for signs of the partner's mood. Scanning for tension, irritation, or withdrawal. Interpreting neutral facial expressions as annoyance. Discounting moments of warmth or openness as "temporary."

### The Belief

The belief that seems to be operating here is something like: **"If I don't keep the peace, I'll lose the relationship."** The user refined this to: "If I show that I need something, I become a burden, and people leave burdens behind."

### Walking Forward

#### Perceptions

With this belief active, the user scans for any sign that they might be "too much." A partner checking their phone becomes evidence. A brief silence becomes proof of withdrawal.

#### Thoughts

"See? They're already pulling away. Better not make it worse."

#### Emotions

Anxiety, followed by a resigned sadness.

#### Feelings

The chest tightness returns. Energy drops. A sense of heaviness.

#### Decisions

Stay quiet. Manage the partner's experience. Be easy, be low-maintenance, be fine.

#### Actions

Say "I'm fine." Change the subject. Smile.

#### Consequences

The issue doesn't get addressed. Resentment accumulates. The partner senses something is off but can't get past "I'm fine." Distance grows. Intimacy decreases.

#### Experiences

The user interprets the growing distance as confirmation: "See? Even when I don't say anything, they're still pulling away. Imagine how bad it would be if I actually said something."

### The Full Loop

The belief that showing needs makes you a burden leads to hypervigilance for signs of rejection, which generates thoughts predicting catastrophe, triggering fear and shame, which produces a bracing sensation in the body, driving an automatic decision to stay silent, resulting in suppressed needs, accumulating resentment, growing distance, and an experience interpreted as proof that the relationship is fragile — reinforcing the original belief that speaking up would make things worse.

### What I Noticed

- The predicted catastrophe (partner leaving, big fight) has rarely or never actually happened, but the belief treats it as inevitable. The prediction is doing more work than the evidence.
- The strategy (staying silent to protect the relationship) is producing the exact outcome the user fears (distance and disconnection). The loop is self-defeating.
- The deeper belief ("needing something makes me a burden") may predate this relationship. The user mentioned their family valued being "low-maintenance" and "not making waves."
- The user can articulate the pattern clearly — awareness is already present. What's missing is not insight but permission.

## Experiment

**Try this:** The next time something small bothers you — not the biggest issue, a small one — say what you're feeling instead of "I'm fine." You might say: "Actually, that comment earlier bothered me a little. Can we talk about it?"

**Notice:** What happens in your body when you consider saying it. What actually happens when you say it. How the other person actually responds versus how you predicted they'd respond.

**When:** This week. Pick something small — the stakes should feel manageable.

**Report back:** Next time we talk, I'd be curious to hear what happened — especially whether the predicted catastrophe materialized or not.
